Biology

Biological Molecules

  • Main nutrients include carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, vitamins, minerals, and water.
  • Carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins are complex organic molecules that often require digestion.
  • Common elements in living organisms are carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen.
  • Food is needed for energy, heat, growth, repair, and maintenance of a healthy body.

Digestive System

  • The alimentary canal includes the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus.
  • Main stages of nutrition are ingestion, digestion, absorption, and egestion.
  • Physical digestion breaks food into smaller pieces; chemical digestion uses enzymes.
  • Salivary amylase digests starch into maltose.

Defence Against Infectious Disease

  • Red blood cells transport oxygen using haemoglobin.
  • White blood cells defend the body: lymphocytes produce antibodies, phagocytes engulf pathogens.
  • Platelets help in blood clotting, and plasma transports dissolved substances.
  • Vaccines help the body develop immunity against specific pathogens.

Chemistry

Atomic Structure

  • Atoms contain equal numbers of protons and electrons, so they are neutral.
  • Proton number is the number of protons in the nucleus.
  • Nucleon number is the total number of protons and neutrons.
  • Number of neutrons = nucleon number - proton number.

Ions and Ionic Compounds

  • An ion has unequal numbers of protons and electrons, so it has a charge.
  • Metals tend to lose electrons to form positive ions called cations.
  • Non-metals tend to gain electrons to form negative ions called anions.
  • Noble gases are stable and usually do not gain or lose electrons.

Acids and Bases

  • Acids react with bases to form salt and water.
  • A base is a metal oxide or metal hydroxide that reacts with an acid.
  • An alkali is a soluble base.
  • Metal + acid gives salt + hydrogen; carbonate + acid gives salt + water + carbon dioxide.

Geography

Tectonic Hazards

  • The Earth has four main layers: inner core, outer core, mantle, and crust.
  • Tectonic plates are large sections of the lithosphere.
  • Volcanoes and earthquakes are linked to plate boundaries and tectonic movement.
  • Hazards include pyroclastic flows, lahars, seismic waves, and aftershocks.

Maths

Key Maths Structure

  • Linear graphs: horizontal lines, vertical lines, and straight-line equations.
  • Simultaneous equations: elimination, substitution, graphical method, and word problems.
  • Expansion and factorisation: distributive law, special products, and difference of two squares.
  • Quadratics: solving by factorisation, graphs, turning points, and word problems.
  • Algebraic fractions: simplification, multiplication, division, LCD, addition, and subtraction.

Linear Graphs and Simultaneous Equations

  • Horizontal lines have equations of the form y = c.
  • Vertical lines have equations of the form x = a.
  • Simultaneous equations can be solved by elimination, substitution, or graphical method.
  • In elimination, remove one variable first, then substitute back to find the other.
  • In substitution, express one variable in terms of the other, then substitute.
  • In graphical solving, the solution is the point of intersection of the two lines.
  • For word problems, define variables clearly, form two equations, solve, then conclude with units.

Expansion and Special Products

  • Use the distributive law to expand brackets.
  • Like terms can be combined only if they have the same variable part.
  • Special identities include (a + b)^2, (a - b)^2, and (a + b)(a - b).
  • Difference of two squares: a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b).
  • Always simplify fully after expansion or factorisation.

Quadratic Equations and Graphs

  • Quadratic equations can often be solved by factorisation and the zero product principle.
  • Do not divide away a variable factor, or you may lose a solution.
  • Quadratic graphs are curves with a turning point.
  • A minimum curve opens upwards and a maximum curve opens downwards.
  • The line of symmetry is a vertical line passing through the turning point.
  • Use a table of values to plot the graph accurately.
  • In quadratic word problems: define, form equation, solve, check, reject invalid roots if needed, and conclude.

Algebraic Fractions

  • To simplify algebraic fractions, factorise numerator and denominator first.
  • Cancel only common factors, not individual terms.
  • For multiplication, multiply across and simplify where possible.
  • For division, flip the second fraction and change division to multiplication.
  • For addition and subtraction, first find the LCM or LCD of the denominators.
  • Then rewrite each fraction with the common denominator before combining numerators.
